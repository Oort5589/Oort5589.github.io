Titanic - Trends in Mortality of Passengers¶

In [1]:
import pandas as pd
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import seaborn as sns

Question?¶

Looking at the data set provided by the Kaggle site for the "Titanic: Machine Learning from Disaster" project I'm struck by the question, what sorts of variables may have influenced the survivability of passengers on that tragic voyage? There were likely a great deal many more factors than what are contained in the provided data, but I think some of the variables that we can look at may very well have at least some marginal effect on survivability.

Potential factors affecting survivability?¶

The main variables I am personally interested in are age, gender, whether they came alone or not, and also socio-economic status. I chose to focus on these since I believe them to be the most likely to have an effect on ultimate survivability.

Wrangling process?¶

After loading in the csv as a Pandas dataframe, I remove passenger Id and name since identifying information would have next to nothing to do with survivability. I also dropped ticket information and embark location. I set up a number of filters to use with our dataframe in order to explore and observe the variables we're interested in, and also as a way to filter out some null values.

Regarding Gender¶

As far as gender relates to survivability, we can see a clear disparity. This is more than apparent just looking at the numbers, and also if we compare them graphically. 74.2% of female passengers survived, while a paltry 18.9% of male passengers survived.
This is a staggering difference, but one which can easily be explained. Most of us have likely seen or read some kind of media adaptation of the Titanic story, the best known probably being the 1997 movie with Leonardo DiCaprio. One of the big recurring threads in most, if not all, of those is that women and children were to be loaded onto lifeboats first.
What is of particular interest is that on that fateful night, two ship's officers were given the order to organize the ship's evacuation and that women and children were to be first. They split and went to either side of the boat with their own interpretation of those orders. One interpreted this to mean that no men were to be loaded in boats, while the other allowed men if there were no additional women or children to load before sending off a boat. This no doubt helps explain the severe disparity between survival rates of both gender groups, apart from just the focus on women and children.

Regarding Family¶

In terms of whether having family with you on the trip has an effect on survivability, there doesn't seem to be quite as drastic a difference between both groups as we saw when looking at gender. There does appear to be a slight advantage in survivability for those with family, but whose to say that's not just a fluke. Did having family help?

To answer the above question, we can compare the means of either groups' "Survived" column to see if there is any statistically meaningful difference between groups.

$H_0: \mu_f - \mu_n = d_0$
$H_a: \mu_f - \mu_n \neq d_0$

Using an $\alpha$ of $.05$, we find we can safely reject the null at the $95\%$ confidence level, according to our t-statistic $(2.8863)$, which is obviously well above the relevant t-value $(1.960)$.
We can safely reject the null hypothesis and say that there is in fact a statistically significant difference in survivability. This is very likely due to the prioritization of women and children during the ship's evacuation, and with the exception of upper class children traveling with an unrelated caregiver, children on board would likely be traveling with their mothers.

Regarding Age¶

Age is something you'll see I chose to look at in a variety of ways. I produced histograms and boxplots to show the age distributions of those surviving and those not. I also put age observations in discrete categories and compared the raw numbers. What I've learned from this is that survivors tended to be younger than those whom perished. Minors (defined as being younger than 18 here) tended to have a higher chance of surviving than their adult peers. This is likely explained by the afore-mentioned "women and children first".

Regarding Socioeconomic Status¶

Looking at socioeconomic status, the disparity between groups is significant, though not as drastic as when we looked at gender. We can see a clear relationship between class and survivability that only gets worse the lower on the social ladder one is.
This makes sense if we look at a diagram of the ship, or really any cruise liner. The nicer, pricier rooms, affordable only to the more affluent, are either above deck or nearby which would put them in closer proximity to lifeboats. Third class fared the worst of all likely in part due to being cabined the farthest below deck.

Conclusion¶

Looking at the data provided, there are some very clear trends in whom survived the sinking of the Titanic. We know that the order was given to evacuate women and children first aboard the Titanic, and this is very clear when comparing the survival rates of men and women. This is also seen looking at the age distributions for survivors and non-survivors. Survivors tended to be younger. This is all not terribly surprising, but what I did find to be of particular interest was that the survival rate went down with socioeconomic status. First class passengers fared quite well, which makes sense as they cabined above deck, which would put them far closer to the lifeboats than their lower class peers whom cabined below deck. Third class passengers naturally fared the worst as they were farthest below deck.

It should be said that, despite these clear trends, there are a number of limitations. There are a fair number of records with one or more missing fields. This is most noticeable when looking at age. I chose to exclude those records whilst looking at age and when using .describe() which may have the unintended effect of skewing my findings one way or the other depending on what those missing values are supposed to be.
